Pink Oregon Trail

A game of westward journey · 1848

✦ ✦ ✦

Welcome, pioneer. The year is 1848. Your wagon train sets out from Independence, Missouri for Oregon City — 2,000 miles away across plains, mountains, deserts, and rivers. You will choose seven brave souls to come with you. You will face cholera, bandits, river crossings, and weather. Some of you may not make it. But those who do will see the Pacific.

What you'll learn

The Oregon Trail is the most famous overland migration in American history. Between 1840 and 1860, between 300,000 and 500,000 people walked west on this trail. About one in ten died along the way. This game lets you experience their journey — the choices they faced, the hardships they endured, and the ingenuity that helped them survive.

What's in the box

  • The game board — a foldable map of the trail, with 25 stops (Short Journey) or 50 stops (Extended Journey)
  • Wagon tokens — one per team, to mark your position on the board
  • The game itself — opens in any web browser, no internet required
  • This rules booklet — what you're reading now

Setting Out

Setup — about 5 minutes

Step 1 — Open the game

Double-click pink_oregon_trail.html on your computer. The game opens in any web browser. No internet required. The game will run from a USB drive if you don't have it on the school computer.

Step 2 — Choose your difficulty

Easy

Forgiving trail. More money. One revival.

Medium

The honest trail. Recommended for most plays.

Hard

Trail of Tears. Cholera spreads. The land is unforgiving.

Step 3 — Choose your mode

  • Single Player — one driver, one wagon, one journey
  • Cooperative — everyone shares one wagon and decides together. Best for first plays.
  • Competitive — 2-4 wagons race west. First to Oregon wins (but survival matters more than speed).

Step 4 — Choose your trail length

  • Short Journey — 25 stops, about 15 minutes — fits a class period
  • Extended Journey — 50 stops, about 40 minutes — the full crossing

Step 5 — Build your team of seven

Seven travelers — different skills, different stories

You will see 23 people who want to come west. Pick exactly seven. Each one brings a skill. The Doctor heals the sick. The Hunter brings food. The Carpenter fixes the wagon. The Priest holds the team's faith. Choose carefully — once you're on the trail, you can't recruit more.

Step 6 — Name them

Give each of the seven a name. Real names — your family, your friends, your favorite characters. They will appear in your story when the journey is done. The trail remembers.

First time playing? Pick the Doctor and the Hunter for sure. The others are up to you.

A Turn at a Time

How to play — every turn, every choice

Each turn, your team picks ONE of four actions. Then the day passes. Then something happens — sometimes ordinary, sometimes not.

The Four Actions

Push On

Roll the dice and travel west. The default action — most turns will be Push On.

Hunt

Spend the day hunting. Brings food, and a hide if you have a Hunter or Trapper.

Rest

Make camp. Heals weakened and sick members. Costs a day of travel.

Forage

Search for berries, plants, and water. Slower than hunting but works in any region.

What happens after you choose

  1. The dice roll. If you Push On, dice decide how far you travel that day.
  2. The day passes. Your team eats food, drinks water, uses supplies. Numbers tick down.
  3. Something happens. The new space might be a fort, a river, a landmark, or just open trail. Often, an event happens — a storm, a stranger, a chance encounter. Choose how to respond.
  4. The next day begins.

The four actions repeat every turn. Push On is the default — you'll do it most days. Hunt when you're low on food and have plenty of time. Rest when someone is sick and you're safe. Forage when you can't decide between Hunt and Rest.

Money ($)
Dollars and cents. Pays for everything at forts.
Wagon HP (0-100)
Damage reduces it. Repairs cost money.
Morale
Team spirit (0-10).

Prices at forts (Medium difficulty, before Merchant discount)

  • Food — $0.12 per pound
  • Water — $0.10 per gallon
  • Medicine — $3.50 per dose
  • Supplies — $1.20 per unit
  • Wagon repair — $4.00 (restores 30 HP)
  • Major repair — $10.00 (restores 50 HP, broken axle)
  • Ferry crossing — $6.00
  • Local guide — $4.00 (avoids the next "lost trail")
  • Visit fort doctor — $5.00 (only when a member is dying; +50% revival chance)
  • Send a letter home — $1.00 (+2 morale)

A Merchant on the team buys at -15% and sells at +40%.

Unexpected costs

  • Food spoils. Water spills. Wheels crack. About one unexpected event per 4-5 days of travel.
  • A Cook halves food spoilage; a Blacksmith reduces wagon damage; a Carpenter rebuilds broken axles for free; a Guide or Scout may avoid the day's mishap altogether.
  • Without medicine, sickness kills.

How to get more of each:

  • Food: Hunt (+22 lbs, Hunter), trap (+12 lbs, Trapper), forage (+10 lbs, Farmer), buy at forts, certain events
  • Water: Cross rivers, forage, buy at forts, certain events
  • Supplies: Buy at forts, certain events
  • Medicine: Buy at forts ($3.50/dose); a Doctor on team starts with +1 dose
  • Hides: Successful hunts (more if Hunter or Trapper on team)
  • Money: Sell hides at forts, sell extra food/water/supplies, day labor at forts, profession-specific encounter events, discovered caches; a Banker on team starts with +$12
  • Wagon HP: Repair at fort ($4 / $10), or free with a Carpenter on team
  • Morale: Rest, send a letter home, certain events, eating well

When a team member's health bar turns gold or orange, act. Rest. Buy food. Find a Doctor. If you wait until red, you may run out of time. The game will warn you — but you have to listen.

If someone is dying

When a team member is in danger of dying, the game will pause and offer you choices:

  • Race to a fort for medical help — costs a day, +30% chance to save them. Hidden when you're already at one.
  • Visit the fort doctor — $5.00, only available when the wagon is currently at a fort. Strong odds (+50%) and no day lost.
  • Stop and tend to them — costs 2 days, doubled with a Doctor on team
  • Pray and continue — slower, requires a Priest for best chance
  • Continue at full pace — last resort, riskiest
  • Use a Revival (Easy mode only — once per game)
A Doctor on your team can save sick and dying members — but only if the Doctor is healthy themselves. Protect your protectors.

Special Situations

Rivers, forts, weather, bandits, and history

Crossing rivers

You will cross many rivers. Each time, you have four choices:

Ford

Walk through. Free but risky if deep.

Caulk

Seal wagon and float. Can damage supplies.

Ferry

Pay 3 Money. Safest option.

Native Guide

Local guide leads you safely. Better with Language Teacher.

Stopping at forts

Forts are critical. At any fort you can:

  • Buy supplies — Money for food/water/supplies
  • Sell goods — Hides and excess become Money
  • Hire on for the day — Earn money but lose travel time
  • Repair wagon — Restore Movement (free if you have a Carpenter)
  • Rest — Spend a day. Heals team. Boosts morale.
  • Get trail news — See what's coming up next
  • Send a letter home — 1 Money, +2 Morale, write to family
  • Move on — Skip the fort and keep going

Storms, animals, and chance

The trail is unpredictable. Storms hit. Wolves circle the camp. A friendly Native family might trade with you. A traveling preacher might bless your journey. A gold prospector might pass through. You'll see a "Something Unexpected" banner when surprise events happen — listen carefully and choose well.

The bandit ambush

Near the end of the trail, you will be ambushed by bandits. Your team will face 3 to 7 multiple-choice questions (depending on difficulty) drawn from historical facts the game showed you along the journey. Pay attention to the Did You Know cards — that's how you win this fight. Get most right, and the bandits ride away. Get most wrong, and you'll lose people and supplies.

Historical landmarks

Special places along the trail (Independence Rock, Chimney Rock, Soda Springs, Fort Laramie, The Dalles) have real historical pictures and facts. These are real places real pioneers passed by. Look at the picture. Read the fact. The bandit quiz will be drawn from these.

Winning the Trail

Strategy and the spirit of the game

How to win

Reach Oregon City — Stop 25 (Short) or Stop 50 (Extended) — before winter snows close the mountain passes (Day 165). The more team members alive when you arrive, the higher your score.

Scoring

  • +20 points per surviving team member
  • +10 points if you finished before Day 130 (the warning day)
  • +15 points for every fort you visited
  • +5 points for every historical fact your team was shown
  • +25 points for the bandit ambush — based on quiz performance
  • Bonus points for high morale, full health, leftover money

Five strategic principles

1. Watch the warning bars. When a team member's health bar drops to gold, act. The game gives you windows — use them.

2. Hides become money. Hunt regularly with a Hunter or Trapper. Sell hides at every fort. Cash flow lets you buy ferries instead of risking river crossings, send letters home, and stock up for hard regions.

3. Rations matter. Bare Bones rations save food but weaken your team — only safe in short bursts. Filling rations heal but burn fast — use after hunts. Meager is the default.

4. Pace matters. Steady is sustainable. Strenuous gets you there faster but tires the team. Grueling is for emergencies only — sustained Grueling pace will collapse your wagon.

5. Pay attention to history. Every Did You Know card might appear in the bandit quiz at the end. The kids who pay attention to the trail win the trail.

What makes a great wagon

Not the one that arrives fastest. Not the one with all seven members alive. The one whose journey was worth telling — the team that made hard choices, lost good people, learned from forts, traded with strangers, and arrived in Oregon with a story.

After the journey, the game will write you a Trail Journal — a multi-paragraph period-voice story of your specific wagon's specific journey. Print it out. Keep it. Read it back to your class.

One last thing

Real pioneers were not just men with rifles. One in five was a child. About half were women. Many were Black, Mexican, or Native American. They came from every faith and every state. They fought, helped, married, mourned, and kept walking. The trail is their story.

This game is yours now. Walk it well.
