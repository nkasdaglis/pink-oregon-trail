Make Your Own Oregon Trail Map

A Pink Oregon Trail project for sixth-grade explorers

You are about to draw the same trail two thousand pioneers walked in 1848. By the time you are done, you will have a real wall-sized map you can play the game on, hang in your room, or take to school. You don't need to be good at drawing. You just need to be patient. The pioneers were patient too.

What you'll need (the outfitting list)

Poster boardOne sheet, 22 × 28 inches works great. Bigger is fine. Tan or cream-colored if you can find it. Plain white works too — you'll paint over the white.
PencilSoft (No. 2). For the planning lines you'll erase later.
EraserThe kneaded kind doesn't tear paper.
Ruler or yardstickFor straight measuring lines. A piece of string also works for curves.
Watercolor or tempera paintYou'll need brown, dark blue, light blue, green, tan/yellow, and a tiny bit of pink.
A round paintbrushMedium size. One brush is fine if you wash it between colors.
Crayons or colored pencilsFor the small details after the paint dries: stop names, labels, and the compass.
A black fine-tip markerFor writing the place names so they read crisp.
A teacup or small jarTo trace circles for the trail stops. The rim should be roughly an inch across.

The plan, before you start

Before you put any pencil to the poster, look at the printed Pink Oregon Trail poster (the 36 × 24 one) for one full minute. Notice three things:

  1. The trail itself is a pink dashed line that curves from the right side (Independence, Missouri) to the left (Oregon City). It is not a straight line.
  2. Mountains are little triangles with white tops. Rivers are squiggly blue lines. Trees and prairie are color washes, not detail.
  3. The biggest stops — forts, the start, the finish — are bigger circles with names. Other stops are smaller. The map breathes; it doesn't try to label everything.

Step-by-step

1

Lay down a grid in pencil.

Lightly draw three horizontal pencil lines across the poster, and four vertical pencil lines top to bottom. This makes a 4 by 4 grid. The lines are just so you know where things go — you will erase them at the end.

Reading the grid: Independence (the start) goes in the top-right square. Oregon City (the finish) goes in the top-left square. The trail runs diagonally across the upper half of the poster.
2

Wash the background with tan paint.

Mix a little brown with lots of water and a small drop of yellow. The wash should be very thin — you should still see your pencil lines through it after it dries. Brush it across the whole poster in long horizontal strokes. Let it dry completely. Old pioneers' maps had this color — it's called parchment.

3

Paint in the regions, lightly.

Once the parchment is dry, mix three more wash colors:

  • Pale green for the Great Plains (the right third of your map — lots of grass)
  • Pale orange-tan for the Great Basin desert (the middle, where the trail goes through what is now Wyoming, Utah, Idaho)
  • Pale blue-green for the Pacific Northwest forests (the left side, where Oregon Country starts)

Brush each color lightly over its area. The colors should be soft — the kind of color you'd see if you held a piece of stained glass up to a window. Let the whole thing dry again.

4

Draw the rivers in dark blue.

The big rivers your wagon will cross are:

  • Missouri River — runs along the right edge of the map, top to bottom
  • Platte River — goes across the middle, splitting into North and South Platte
  • Snake River — squiggles across the middle-left, west of the Rockies
  • Columbia River — runs east-to-west across the top of the left third, ending at the Pacific Ocean

Use a thin brush. Make the rivers wavy, not straight. Real rivers wander.

5

Paint the mountain ranges as little brown triangles with white tops.

The pioneers had to cross or go around three big ranges:

  • The Rocky Mountains — a band of triangles in the middle of the map. Use brown for the body and put a tiny dab of white on the very top of each one for snow.
  • The Blue Mountains — a smaller band in the upper-left section, in what is now eastern Oregon.
  • The Cascade Range — near the Pacific coast, on the far left. Mt. Hood and Mt. Rainier are the famous ones — make those triangles taller.
Don't try to draw real mountains. Old maps used a row of overlapping triangles. It looks more like a crown than a photograph. That's the right look.
6

Trace and number the trail stops.

This is the most important step. Use the teacup to trace 25 small circles for the trail stops. Use the table on the next page to know where each circle goes (which grid square) and what color to paint it.

Number each stop from 1 to 25 with a black marker. Write the number small, inside the circle.

7

Draw the pink trail, dashed.

Connect the 25 circles with a curving pink line. The line should be dashed — not one long line, but short pink lines about half an inch long with a gap between them. Make the line curve a little; the pioneers didn't walk in a straight line.

This is why the game is called Pink Oregon Trail. The trail is pink because the wildflowers along the prairie were sometimes pink — and because Gabby asked for a pink trail.

8

Add the seven piece zones.

Next to each fort, plus next to Independence and Oregon City, draw a small dotted square. These are piece zones — this is where you park your wagon piece when you stop. They should be about two inches across. Use a dotted line so they don't fight with the rest of the map.

9

Letter the names.

With your fine-tip black marker, write the name of every fort, the start, and the finish next to its circle. Don't write the names of the smaller stops — they will crowd the map. The number on the circle is enough.

Why not label every stop? Old maps left small places to be discovered. Plus, when you play the game, the screen will tell you the name of every spot you land on. The map just needs to show you where you are.
10

Add a compass rose.

In one of the empty corners (bottom-right is good), draw a circle about three inches across. Inside, draw a four-pointed star with N at the top, S at the bottom, E to the right, W to the left. Make N a little fancier — a tall pointed arrow. This is your compass rose. Every old map had one.

11

Add a title cartouche.

In the bottom-left or bottom-center, draw a simple decorative box (rectangle with rounded corners, or a curling banner). Inside, write in your fanciest letters:

A MAP of the OREGON TRAIL
From Independence in Missouri to the Willamette Valley
Drawn by [your name], 1848

Old maps always had this. Pioneers knew their maps were drawings — the cartouche signed and dated the work.

12

Erase the pencil grid lines.

Now that everything is in place, gently erase the four vertical and three horizontal pencil lines from step 1. Be careful not to smudge your paint. If a paint area is still slightly wet, leave that area for last.

13

Sign your work and date it.

In tiny letters in a corner: your name, the date you finished, and "1848." That last part is a tradition — mapmakers always wrote the year of the map's content, not the year they drew it.

Tips from the trail

Mistakes are fine. If a circle is too big, paint over it tan and try again. If a river ends up in the wrong place, just turn it into a creek. Pioneers' real maps had mistakes too.
Don't rush the drying. Watercolor that's still wet will smear when you draw on top. If you only have one afternoon, do steps 1–5 today and 6–13 tomorrow.
Show off the trail itself. The pink trail line is the soul of the map. Give it room. Don't crowd it with too many labels or extra drawings.
Pretend you are a pioneer mapmaker. You are drawing this in 1848 by candlelight after a long day on the wagon. You wouldn't make every line perfect. Loose lines feel right.

Before you start: the checklist

  • Do I have all my paint colors? (brown, two blues, green, tan, pink)
  • Do I have a teacup or jar with about a one-inch rim?
  • Do I have a black fine-tip marker?
  • Do I have my poster board and a flat workspace?
  • Have I looked at the printed Pink Oregon Trail poster for one minute?
  • Do I know where I will hang the finished map?
